We are pleased to submit our manuscript entitled **"The Fragility Atlas: How Robust Are Cochrane Meta-Analysis Conclusions to Analyst Choices? A Large-Scale Multiverse Analysis"** for consideration as a Research Article in *The BMJ*.

**Why this matters for BMJ readers:** Meta-analyses directly inform clinical guidelines, drug approvals, and health policy. Yet every meta-analysis involves methodological choices — which variance estimator, which confidence interval method, whether to correct for publication bias — that are rarely scrutinised. Our study is the first to systematically quantify how sensitive Cochrane meta-analysis conclusions are to these choices at scale.

**What we found:** Across 403 Cochrane systematic reviews and 394,569 individual meta-analytic specifications, we found that 58% of review conclusions changed under reasonable analytical variation. Only 20% were robust across more than 90% of specifications. The single most influential analytical decision — whether to correct for publication bias — explained more variance than all other methodological choices combined (eta-squared = 0.374). Critically, fragility was unpredictable from review characteristics: neither more studies nor larger effects conferred methodological stability (R-squared = 0.018).

**Why this is novel:** While individual dimensions of analytical sensitivity (variance estimators, confidence interval methods, publication bias corrections) have been studied in isolation, no previous study has simultaneously varied all dimensions across hundreds of real meta-analyses. The multiverse analysis framework reveals interactions between analytical choices that single-dimension sensitivity analyses cannot capture.

**Clinical implications:** These findings have direct relevance for GRADE certainty assessments, guideline development, and meta-analytic reporting standards. We propose that "analytical inconsistency" deserves consideration alongside statistical inconsistency (heterogeneity) as a dimension of evidence quality.

The manuscript has not been published or submitted elsewhere. All authors have approved the submitted version. We have no competing interests to declare. The analysis code, dataset, and an interactive dashboard are publicly available.

We believe this work will be of substantial interest to BMJ readers and look forward to your consideration.
